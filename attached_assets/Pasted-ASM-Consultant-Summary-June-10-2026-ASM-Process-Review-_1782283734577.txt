Major Discussion Themes
1. Curation Becomes the New Critical Control Point

Jay immediately focused on the biggest architectural risk.

He separated the future-state process into:

Step 1 — Curation

Creating the canonical ASM dataset.

Step 2 — Transformation

Converting canonical data into payer-specific layouts.

Jay's concern:

Most silent compliance issues occur during curation because data is being transformed before submission.

Consultant Assessment

This was one of the most important observations of the meeting.

Under the current architecture:

Errors are isolated to individual payer processes.

Under the future architecture:

Errors introduced in the curated layer can propagate to every payer.

Samir reinforced this concern by noting that centralized curation creates a single point of failure across all plans.

2. Validation Must Prove Old and New Processes Match

Jennifer described the migration strategy.

Her goal:

Build curated tables
Build staging tables
Generate the same outputs as the current Oracle process
Validate equivalency before optimization

The initial objective is not transformation improvement.

The initial objective is:

Produce the same outputs as today's process and prove parity.

Consultant Observation

This is the correct migration approach.

Phase 1:

Replicate

Phase 2:

Optimize

Phase 3:

Enhance
3. CMS Validation Should Become Explicit

Kate raised a key concern.

She questioned whether current logic is routinely validated against:

CMS CPT lists
CMS acceptable service types
Current CMS requirements

Jennifer indicated some validation exists but is not fully transparent or automated today. Sandra confirmed that integrating CMS requirements into pre-submission validation is a future-state objective.

Recommendation

Future validation should include:

CPT eligibility checks
HCPCS eligibility checks
Provider specialty validation
Bill type validation
Diagnosis validity checks
Effective date validation

before export generation.

4. Major Discussion on "Sweeps"

A large portion of the session focused on a question Jay raised:

Why Is Mercy Structuring ASM Around Sweeps?

Jay challenged the assumption that submissions should be constrained by payer sweep schedules.

His argument:

Mercy should submit validated data continuously.
Plans should handle their own sweep timing.
Providers should not artificially restrict submissions.
Best-Practice Recommendation

Jay and Samir recommended:

Send Data Continuously

Rather than:

July submission:

DOS 7/1/25–6/30/26 only

Use:

All valid open-period records
New records
Retroactive chart reviews
Newly discovered diagnoses

provided duplicates are avoided.

5. Open Period Concept

One of the most important educational discussions occurred around CMS open periods.

Sandra was focused on:

July submission windows
Initial sweep windows

Jay and Samir reframed the discussion.

Example:

If 2025 DOS remains open through January 2027:

Newly discovered diagnoses from March 2025 remain valid for submission.

Therefore:

Submission windows should follow CMS open periods rather than payer sweep boundaries.

Consultant Observation

This represents a maturity shift from:

"Submit by sweep"

to

"Submit all eligible diagnoses during the open period."

6. Need for Historical Submission Inventory

A direct consequence of the above discussion was recognition that Mercy needs submission history.

Jennifer noted that future-state logic will need to know:

What has already been submitted
What has not been submitted
What is retroactive
What is net new

to avoid duplicate submissions.

This aligns directly with the Run Control / Submission Inventory architecture we have discussed previously.

7. Reconciliation Strategy Expanded

Another major topic was response-file reconciliation.

Sandra explained that:

Some plans provide acknowledgements and rejects.
Some plans provide almost no response information.

Samir recommended using CMS files as the authoritative source.

MAO-002

Used to determine:

What the plan actually submitted to CMS
Transaction-level acceptance/rejection
Reject reason codes
MAO-004

Used to determine:

What CMS ultimately accepted for risk adjustment processing

Sandra indicated Mercy currently receives MAO-004 files and Jennifer is developing validation processes around them.

Jay's MAO-004 Playbook (Excel Reference)

Toward the end of the meeting Jay shared an Excel-based MAO-004 playbook.

Sandra reacted positively and indicated it would be extremely useful.

The workbook appears to classify MAO-004 outcomes into:

Accepted encounters
Provider-controlled issues
Plan-controlled issues
Shared responsibility issues
CMS-controlled issues

The purpose is to move beyond simply reading MAO-004 files and instead determine:

Operational Actionability

For each scenario:

Who owns the issue?
Can Mercy fix it?
Does the plan need to act?
Is CMS action required?
Is correction worthwhile?

This is likely the Excel file Jay referenced during the final portion of the discussion.

Consultant Assessment

The June 10 discussion surfaced three strategic findings:

1. Mercy's future-state architecture is directionally correct

Centralized curation plus payer-specific export generation is the right long-term model.

2. Curation becomes the highest-risk component

A defect in the curated layer could affect every payer simultaneously.

3. Mercy needs a formal submission inventory and reconciliation framework

To support:

Open-period submissions
Retroactive coding
Duplicate prevention
MAO-002 reconciliation
MAO-004 reconciliation
Closed-loop acceptance tracking

This discussion is actually the earliest point where the future-state concepts of:

canonical ASM staging,
run control,
reverse reconciliation,
and submission traceability

begin to emerge very clearly.
